Racing Royalty: Where speed meets legacy on the Formula 1 track.

Racing Roots, RC Cars to Karting:
Lewis Hamilton's introduction to motorsport began with remote-controlled (RC) cars, which he raced competitively and even became the runner-up in a national RC racing championship at the age of six. Recognizing his talent, his father Anthony, pondered whether Lewis's skills in RC racing could transfer to full-size motorsport. This curiosity set the stage for Hamilton's transition to karting and marked the beginning of his journey in competitive racing.


Karting Triumphs, A Meteoric Rise:
Lewis Hamilton started karting at eight, winning the British karting championship and STP karting championship two years later. In 1996, he dominated the cadet class, winning the Champions of the Future series, Sky TV KartMasters, and Five Nations Championship. Progressing to Junior Yamaha in 1997, Hamilton continued his success, securing victories in the Champions of the Future and Super One series, reaffirming his status as British Champion.


The Pact, Journey from Autograph to F1 Dream:
At their initial encounter, Ron Dennis inscribed in Lewis Hamilton's autograph book, "Phone me in nine years, we'll sort something out then." True to the promise, in 1998, Hamilton, at the age of 13, became the youngest driver ever to be officially signed to the McLaren Driver Development Support program. This landmark contract not only provided financial and technical backing but also held the potential for a future entry into Formula One.


Accelerating Ambitions, Rise in Junior Karting:
Progressing to the Junior Intercontinental A level in 1998, Lewis Hamilton secured a remarkable second-place finish in the McLaren Mercedes Champions of the Future. His talents extended to the Italian Open Championship, where he claimed a notable fourth position. The following year, in 1999, Hamilton continued his success in Junior Intercontinental A, achieving the status of Vice European Champion, winning the Trophy de Pomposa, and once again finishing impressively in the Italian Open Championship at fourth place.


Karting Triumphs, Global Domination:
In the same year, Lewis Hamilton ventured into Intercontinental A, securing victory in the Italian 'Industrials' Championship. The subsequent karting season proved even more triumphant as he clinched the European Champion title in Formula A by winning all four rounds. Hamilton's achievements extended internationally with victories in the World Cup Championship in Japan, the Elf Masters at Bercy in France, and the second round of the Italian Open. His success garnered recognition from the British Racing Drivers’ Club, which honored him as a 'Rising Star' Member.


Revving Up, Transition to Formula Renault:
Formula Renault served as Lewis Hamilton's entry point into car racing, concluding his successful karting chapter. Despite a rocky start during his first test with Manor Motorsport, where he crashed the Formula Renault at Mallory Park after only three laps, Hamilton's resilience and speed stood out. Unfazed by the incident, he quickly adapted, impressing McLaren with his ability to rebound and perform under pressure.


The Triumph, From Third to Dominance in Formula Renault:
After a promising fifth-place finish in the British Formula Renault Winter Series, Lewis Hamilton entered the full championship in 2002. Despite facing competition from more experienced drivers like Danny Watts, Hamilton secured three wins, finishing third overall. The following year showcased Hamilton’s true prowess as he claimed his first victory in the fifth round at Silverstone. From there, he dominated, winning all but one of the next ten races. Hamilton secured the championship with an impressive tally of ten wins, nine fastest laps, eleven pole positions, and 419 points, outclassing runner-up Alex Lloyd with 371 points, even allowing him to miss the last two races.


Resilience and Triumph, Formula Three Journey:
Lewis Hamilton faced adversity in the final round of the British Formula Three championship with Manor, encountering a crash that led to his hospitalization despite strong qualifying. Undeterred, he returned to Formula Three in 2004, shifting to the Euroseries with Manor. Hamilton secured a victory at the Norising street track, finishing fifth overall behind champions like Jamie Green and future F1 drivers Alexandre Premat, Nicolas Lapierre, and Nico Rosberg. The same year brought additional victories in the Bahrain F3 Superprix and race one of the Macau F3 Grand Prix. Hamilton’s exceptional year concluded with his first test in a McLaren F1 car in December, a testament to his resilience and rising star status.


F3 Mastery, Stellar Year with ASM:
In 2005, Lewis Hamilton’s switch to ASM in Formula Three mirrored his previous dominance in Formula Renault. Remarkably, he won 15 out of 20 races, securing a 16th victory at Spa-Francorchamps (later disqualified for a technical infringement). Hamilton outperformed teammate Adrian Sutil. The same year, he triumphed at the F3 Marlboro Masters in Zandvoort, Netherlands, clinched both Monaco F3 Grand Prix races and both Pau F3 Grand Prix races, all from pole position. This exceptional year solidified Hamilton’s reputation as a formidable force in Formula Three.


GP2 Triumph, A Stepping Stone to Formula 1:
Lewis Hamilton's journey to Formula 1 took a decisive turn through GP2, where he joined champions ART. Facing rivals like Premat and his main challenger, Nelson Piquet Jnr, Hamilton showcased dominance, securing victories at Nurburgring and Monte-Carlo. His pivotal move at Silverstone left the crowd in awe, dealing a crucial blow to Piquet. Despite challenges, Hamilton clinched the GP2 title in unusual circumstances at Monza, setting the stage for his graduation to Formula 1.


F1 Debut:
In 2007, McLaren faced a vacant slot alongside Fernando Alonso. Surprising many, including the departure of experienced drivers Raikkonen and Montoya, team principal Ron Dennis chose rookie Lewis Hamilton over seasoned competitors like de la Rosa and Paffett. The decision, kept under wraps until November 24th, aimed to avoid overshadowing Michael Schumacher’s retirement. Dennis cited Hamilton’s long-standing association with McLaren and his deserving talent as the key factors behind the unconventional choice. Hamilton’s Spectacular Debut Season in Formula 1 catapulted him to instant fame. Remarkably, he secured a podium finish in his very first race and maintained this impressive streak for nine consecutive races. His outstanding performance included notable victories in Montreal and Indianapolis, showcasing his exceptional talent early in his career, eventually finishing second in the Drivers’ Championship.


First F1 Win:
Lewis Hamilton's first Formula 1 Grand Prix victory occurred on June 10, 2007, at the Circuit Gilles Villeneuve in Montreal, Canada. He was driving for the McLaren-Mercedes team during the Canadian Grand Prix. This win marked a significant milestone in Hamilton's career as it was his sixth race in Formula 1, and this victory was a precursor to his subsequent successes in the sport.


First F1 Championship:
Staying with McLaren in 2008, Hamilton faced challenges with Alonso’s departure and Kovalainen’s arrival. Despite a disappointing loss in the 2007 championship, Hamilton started the new season strong, winning the opener in Melbourne. However, his second season saw lapses in composure, marked by serious mistakes in Bahrain, Canada, France, and Japan. Amidst these struggles, Hamilton showcased brilliance in rain-soaked races, notably dominating at Silverstone. Controversies surrounded him, with five penalties throughout the season, including a post-race disqualification at Spa. Overcoming setbacks, he triumphed in China, setting the stage for a nail-biting title showdown in Interlagos. Hamilton’s ultra-conservative strategy secured the championship by a single point, marking a dramatic end to the season, becoming the youngest World Champion at that time.


Challenges and Redemption, 2009:
Facing a slow start with the MP4-24, McLaren struggled in the 2009 season, making it clear early on that Hamilton wouldn’t defend his title. Complications escalated when they were accused of deception during the first race, leading to Hamilton’s disqualification and a public apology in Malaysia. The car’s performance improved significantly in the latter half of the season, allowing Hamilton to secure victories in Hungary and Singapore. Despite the initial setbacks, he finished the year in a commendable fifth place in the championship, trailing behind the dominant Brawn and Red Bull drivers.


Resilience in 2010, Pursuit for Championship Glory:
Equipped with the competitive MP4-25, Hamilton made a strong comeback in the 2010 season. Leading at the halfway mark with victories in Turkey and Canada, he secured a third win in Belgium. However, setbacks in Italy and Singapore, where collisions ended his races, posed challenges to his championship campaign. Despite staying in contention until the final round and finishing second, Hamilton concluded the season in fourth place in the championship. His resilient performance showcased his determination to vie for championship glory.


Struggles in 2011, Wins Amidst Turmoil:
In the 2011 Formula 1 season dominated by Vettel, Hamilton faced a tumultuous campaign marked by errors and incidents. Despite the scrappiness, he clinched three victories. Beginning with promise in Melbourne, where he secured second place, Hamilton faced challenges with the new MP4-26 and encountered unnecessary incidents throughout the season. While he showcased moments of brilliance, such as a consummate victory in Germany, Hamilton often found himself in collisions and controversies. Button’s ability to extract similar performance from the Pirelli tires with less wear became evident. Despite finishing the season behind his teammate and facing penalties from the stewards, Hamilton’s resilience shone through in select moments of racing excellence.


Challenges and Transition in 2012 Season with McLaren:
Despite the MP4-27’s competitiveness in 2012, Hamilton’s season was marred by reliability issues and operational errors, taking him out of realistic championship contention early on. Struggling to convert pole positions into victories, he faced setbacks, including starting from the back in Spa after running out of fuel in qualifying. While Hamilton secured wins in Canada, Hungary, Italy, and America, he often found himself out of the points due to circumstances beyond his control. As his five-year deal with McLaren approached its end, speculation about his future intensified. Surprising many, Hamilton announced his departure from McLaren after the Singapore Grand Prix, signaling a significant move to join Mercedes.


F1 Mercedes Era:
This partnership proved to be immensely successful, with Hamilton clinching multiple Drivers' Championships and contributing to Mercedes' dominance in the Constructors' Championship.


Strategic Shift in 2013, Transition to Mercedes:
In a pivotal move, Hamilton ended prolonged speculation in September by confirming his shift to Mercedes for the 2013 season. This decision proved timely, as Mercedes outperformed McLaren, securing second place in the world championship. While Mercedes won three times that year, Hamilton’s lone victory came in Hungary. Despite facing challenges with tire wear in the first half of the season and struggling with the car’s balance, Hamilton secured pole position five times. Although not entirely satisfied with his car, he narrowly outscored teammate Nico Rosberg in the latter half of the season, marking a solid start to his journey with Mercedes.


Triumph Amidst Challenges in 2014 Season with Mercedes:
In a season marked by doubts, Hamilton’s move to Mercedes proved wise as the W05 dominated the 2014 championship, securing his second title. However, challenges emerged early with an engine failure in the first race. Despite reclaiming the points lead from Rosberg with four consecutive wins, Monaco saw controversy over a yellow flag incident. Further obstacles arose, including brake failures and car issues in qualifying. Hamilton’s resilience shone as he fought back to win races, notably in Belgium after a puncture incident. The controversial double points finale heightened the title race, but Hamilton’s lead prevailed, securing the championship. A final-race Mercedes reliability problem eliminated Rosberg, cementing Hamilton’s triumphant 2014 season.


His Dominance, Securing Back-to-Back Championships in 2015:
In 2015, Hamilton not only retained his championship but also equaled Ayrton Senna’s record and surpassed his hero’s win tally. Addressing a previous weakness, Hamilton started from pole position 11 times in the first 12 races, converting most into victories and securing the championship early. Despite a scrappy performance in Hungary and a technical failure for Rosberg in Italy, Hamilton’s advantage in the championship remained. A crucial moment occurred in Russia, where Rosberg faced another breakdown, virtually sealing the championship for Hamilton. An unforced error by Rosberg in the following race solidified Hamilton’s title. Though Rosberg rallied in the next races, winning each from pole position, and Monaco brought an unexpected pit stop disappointment for Hamilton, these were minor setbacks in an overwhelmingly successful campaign.


Champion’s Unfortunate Fall 2016 Season:
In a surprising turn of events, Hamilton fell short in retaining his title during his fourth season at Mercedes, despite the team’s consistent performance advantage. Numerous power unit problems and a shocking failure in Malaysia disrupted his season. While Hamilton won ten races, outpacing his teammate, Rosberg’s better reliability and consistent performance proved decisive. Hamilton’s bid for the title in the final races required wins and Rosberg’s absence from the podium, an unlikely scenario. Ultimately, Rosberg secured the championship, and his subsequent departure from the sport added another twist to the 2016 F1 season.


Triumph Amid Challenges, 2017 F1 Season Summary:
Following Nico Rosberg’s sudden retirement, Mercedes swiftly enlisted Valtteri Bottas as Lewis Hamilton’s new partner. The 2017 season began with a formidable challenge from Ferrari, with Sebastian Vettel winning the opening race. Hamilton faced setbacks, including a headrest failure in Azerbaijan and a contentious incident with Vettel in the same race. Bottas contributed wins in Russia and Austria. Post-summer break, Hamilton’s dominance surged, winning in Belgium, Italy, and Singapore. Technical issues for Ferrari boosted Hamilton’s lead, culminating in his fourth world title with a ninth-place finish in Mexico, overcoming early-season hurdles for a championship that initially seemed closely contested.


2018 Triumph Overcoming Challenges on the Road to Victory:
Lewis Hamilton successfully defended his championship in 2018, though the season posed early challenges. A strategy error in Australia and a tricky start with the W09 made the first races competitive. Victories in Azerbaijan, Austria, and Francx showcased Hamilton’s prowess. Despite setbacks, like a first-lap incident at his home race, he rallied for wins in Germany, Italy, and Singapore. Clinching the title in Mexico, Hamilton continued the momentum with wins in Brazil and Abu Dhabi. With five world championships and 73 race victories, he was closing in on Schumacher’s records.


Commanding Path to Sixth Title in 2019:
Despite Ferrari’s pre-season strength and Bottas winning the first race, Lewis Hamilton swiftly reasserted his dominance, claiming victories and overcoming challenges, notably benefiting from Leclerc’s failure in Bahrain. The W10’s minor qualifying disadvantage didn’t hinder Hamilton’s overall performance as he secured race wins to lead the championship. Ferrari’s pole positions post-summer break weren’t enough to bridge the gap. Hamilton secured his sixth title with victories in Russia and Mexico, solidifying his status as a force to be reckoned with in the 2019 season.


Exceptional 2020 Breaking Records On and Off the Track:
In an unprecedented year shaped by the COVID-19 pandemic, Lewis Hamilton not only navigated the challenges of a disrupted season but also emerged as a vocal advocate for human rights and racial equality during the hiatus. Despite an initial stumble, gifting the first win to Bottas, Hamilton’s focus on and off the track was unmatched. Overcoming the hurdles, he claimed more than half of the race victories, ending the year as the driver with the most Grand Prix wins ever and matching Michael Schumacher with seven world drivers’ championship victories.


Rollercoaster Season, Battling Verstappen, Breaking Records, and the Controversial Title Decider:
Lewis Hamilton’s pursuit of an unprecedented eighth world championship in 2021 faced uncertainty in pre-season testing as Mercedes encountered challenges. Despite this, Hamilton kicked off the season with a win in Bahrain, sparking a season-long duel with Max Verstappen for the championship. Amid record-breaking achievements like his 100th pole position and race victory, Hamilton continued to advocate for social justice, symbolized by adopting the LGBT+ Pride flag on his helmet. Controversy struck with high-speed clashes with Verstappen, notably in Silverstone, Monza, and Saudi Arabia. The championship decider at Yas Marina saw Hamilton in control until a late safety car and contentious decisions led to him losing the lead and the title on the final lap to Verstappen. Mercedes chose not to appeal, and Hamilton, initially congratulating Verstappen, left the circuit without public comment for months.


Historic 100, A Century of Pole Positions in Formula 1:
Lewis Hamilton etched his name in Formula 1 history, becoming the first driver to achieve an unparalleled 100 pole positions ahead of the Spanish Grand Prix. Hamilton’s milestone sets him far above the rest, with Michael Schumacher’s 68 poles being the next closest. Remarkably consistent, mirroring his longevity in race victories. The emotional significance of this achievement was evident as Hamilton compared the jubilation to his first pole in 2007, marking a truly iconic moment in his illustrious career.


Victory in His 100th Grand Prix, a Historic F1 Achievement:
His historic 100th Formula 1 victory at the 2021 Russian Grand Prix marked a milestone in motorsport history. With unparalleled skill, determination, and strategic brilliance, Hamilton secured his century of wins, solidifying his legacy as one of the greatest drivers in the sport. His remarkable journey continues to redefine excellence on the racetrack, inspiring fans worldwide and etching his name in the annals of Formula 1 greatness.


Resilience in 2022 Navigating Challenges and Future Considerations:
Returning with Mercedes amid uncertainty, Lewis Hamilton faced a transformed Formula 1 landscape in 2022 due to new technical regulations. Mercedes struggled to regain its former dominance, securing their first win only in the penultimate round courtesy of Hamilton’s teammate, George Russell. Hamilton’s season began with a podium in Bahrain, but Mercedes faced challenges, going seven races without a podium. Upgrades improved their performance, leading to five consecutive podiums for Hamilton. Although the season had its share of struggles, including a sixth-place finish in the standings, Hamilton’s determination and discussions about extending his Mercedes contract beyond 2023 reflected his ongoing commitment to the sport.


Mercedes’ Disappointing 2023 F1 Season His Struggle and Red Bull Dominance:
In this season, Lewis Hamilton’s early realization about the shortcomings of Mercedes’ W14 proved accurate as the team faced a second consecutive win-less year. Despite some podium finishes, the car’s inconsistent performance and struggles against Red Bull led to Hamilton finishing a distant third in the drivers’ standings, trailing behind Max Verstappen by a substantial 341 points.
